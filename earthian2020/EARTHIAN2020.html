 4.What are the categories of waste ?

☆ In general, the wastes may be classified into the following categories: 

  • Solid wastes – These are the unwanted substances that are discarded by human society. These include urban wastes, industrial wastes, agricultural wastes, biomedical wastes and radioactive wastes.
  • Liquid wastes – Wastes generated from washing, flushing or manufacturing processes of industries are called liquid wastes.
  • Gaseous wastes – These are the wastes that are released in the form of gases from automobiles, factories or burning of fossil fuels like petroleum. They get mixed in the other gases atmosphere and occasionally cause events such as smog and acid rain.

5.What happens when you mix different types of waste like biomedical waste and hazardous wastes ?

☆ Improper segregation and disposal of biomedical waste has the potential to contaminate groundwater sources, which in turn may infect humans and animals alike.

 Hazardous wastes affect the developing organs of foetus and  young child which gets permanently damaged. If bio medical waste mix with hazardous waste, new diseases and new microorganisms may provoke leading to human destruction on a large scale.

6. Why is it important to segregate our waste? Do you segregate your wastes into wet and dry wastes separately?

☆Segregation is the first step in a compliant waste management plan that will help to save the environment and improve the quality of the atmosphere we live in. Segregation of waste is important 

  • to reduce the use of raw materials
  • to keep recyclable material out of landfills and
  • to keep incompatible garbage separate from other

            Yes, we segregate our waste into wet and Dry at our home. Because in our home, generally waste items like aluminum foils, tetra packs, glass, paper, plastics, metals, etc. fall under the dry waste category, whereas kitchen waste such as stale food, fruits and vegetables come under wet waste.

7.What is biodegradable waste? How can it be used to improve plant growth?

☆Biodegradable waste includes any organic matter in waste which can be broken down into carbon dioxide, water, methane or simple organic molecules by micro-organisms and other living things by composting, aerobic digestion, anaerobic digestion or similar processes.

The biodegradable wastes like egg shell, fruit and vegetable peel, cow dung etc…contains essential micro and macro nutrients that improve the fertility of the soil and yield of the plants. So biodegradable wastes can be used to improve plant growth.

1.Do you think the current waste management in your school is effective?

    ●  Yes, we think the current waste management in our school is effective. Our school doesn't permit the usage of single use plastic items and PET bottles. Our school has sufficient workers to collect waste at daily basis from each classroom. Children are given adequate awareness regarding the waste management, disposal and segregation. We have activities to reuse the waste items in our school on every Saturday. All waste items are disposed in a proper way.

2. Where does the waste go finally from your school bin?

      ●    Our usage --> Trash bin of our school --> Common bin of the area from where the municipal workers collect it --> Material Recovery Facilities --> Landfills or to recycling plants, composters and waste-to-energy plants.

3. Why do we have waste dumps in cities?

         ●   Waste dumps are situated in the cities for waste management resources such as temporary storage, transfer or for various stages of processing waste material, such as sorting, treatment or recycling. It is the most common form of waste disposal.

4. Where are the waste dumps usually located?

          ●    They should be located in silt and clay soils that restrict gas movement because if it is constructed over gravel or sand, it can pose a threat to groundwater quality. It should be accessible by solid waste vehicles in all conditions. The area shouldn't affect the environment. It must comprise enough land and internal capacity. It must not be near any residential areas.

5. What happens when the dumps or landfills fill up? What if your house was near a dumpsite-how would you feel?

              ●   When the landfill has reached its full capacity, the waste is covered with clay and another plastic shield which forms a barrier between trash and surrounding environment. Topsoil is added over the clay. All this is done to get rid of contaminated items inside landfill.  This is known as capping of landfill.

 After almost 30 years, they can be transformed into parks, golf courses, etc.

                ●  We may be affected by lung cancer, respiratory diseases as well as deaths if we reside near a landfill.  We also will be exposed to bad odor.  

6. What are the problems that are associated with the ways in which cities manage their waste?  

             ●  Open dumps release Methane which causes fire, explosions and global warming. Odor is also a serious problem. Discarded tires collect water, allowing mosquitoes to breed, increasing the risk of malaria and dengue. They also cause breathing difficulties. These are some of the problems associated with waste management.

7. Is our current waste management system linear or circular?

           ●Our waste management system has been linear since a long time. This means that we use raw materials from the Earth -->make new products -->throw them into a landfill or incinerate them after a use. It is best if we move to circular very soon.

8. Why do you think waste is managed in those ways currently?

        ●  Waste is managed in linear system but should be moved onto circular system because of the following reasons:

Linear system leads to excess stock in the market which will cause money loss for the company. This also leads to depletion and over-exploitation of natural resources.

 This in turn provokes the accumulation of waste which can be harmful to the environment. E.g.: Plastic

9. How does our current waste management affect our health and the environment?

          ●  It releases toxins such as dioxins and many hazardous gases such as sulfur dioxide, carbon monoxide, methane is also released which causes many ailments and also causes air pollution and acid rain. The ash from incinerators contain heavy toxic metals like cadmium and lead.

10. Do you think the problems in the case studies occur in other parts of the country as well?

                ●  Of course. All the problems mentioned in the case studies occur in other parts of the country as well but in a small scale. Air pollution occurs in almost all cities in India.

The dumping of waste like plastic into oceans is common in most of the coastal states of India. Many hazardous gases are released from the landfills in majority of the states. Even the waste bins overflow that emit a foul smell and also become the breeding place of bacteria and germs. Even rats also cause diseases through these wastes. Burning of waste is also followed in many areas.

A DAY IN THE LIFE OF A

 WASTE WORKER 

1. What kind(s) of waste do they deal with?

They collect mostly all the types of waste that are produced in every house. All the waste is collected together from every house and carried to an immense ground in Kurumaampet.

2. How did they begin this work? Was it by choice or they had no other source of livelihood?

Most of the municipal workers are employed due to their poverty. They are not educated. They had no other option to live their life. Their family was in need of food and basic needs. And hence they have been working to fulfill their basic needs.

3. Do they work alone? Are they self-employed? Contracted to a company or an organisation?

Our team interviewed few persons who work in Municipal corporation of Puducherry. Every day they should collect waste from the allocated areas to receive their daily wages

4. What does their day look like?

Their day is not in their hands. They have to work so hard to fulfill their essential needs. Their family is dependent on his/her wages. The persons whom we interviewed said that if one speaks to them freely without restrictions, that would make their day beautiful and bright.

5. Do they follow the same routine every day?

Yes, they have to collect the garbage waste from the allocated areas daily without fail. They are following this as routine every day

6. Do they collect from a particular area every day?

Yes, they collect waste from specific areas which are prorated to them. Each worker has 2 - 3 areas distributed among them.

7. How do they collect the waste?

They come in the specified vehicle which resembles a lorry. They also come in a small pedalling vehicle in small areas. Once they reach their areas they whistle to make the people aware of that they have arrived to collect waste.

8. Do they have safety regulations that they follow? If not then should these be mandated- why?

Garbage collectors should wear protective gear, including goggles, nose-and-mouth masks, and heavy gloves. Many workers, like Montano, also keep towels and containers of clean water around to scrub themselves. They should wear heavy gloves that resist the cuts as they are disposed without segregation. To avoid the risk of infections especially during this pandemic, they should be tested for Covid 19. They should be also vaccinated regularly. They should be provided with gloves, proper masks, surgical caps for a healthy society

9. What are some of the difficulties they face in the collection of waste? Is the waste segregated?

They face extreme health problems like Diseases from poisonous gases and micro-organisms in the air near garbage dumps. The foul smells released in garbage result in health problems.

They also face . They were respected little before this pandemic. They were considered as dirty, not hygienic and people felt disgusting to come near them. This upsets  the workers a lot. But this pandemic had made them gain all the respect they lost.

10. What kind of waste cannot be recycled?

Not everything can be recycled.

Plastics like toys, styrofoam, bubble wrap are not recycled easily. Some of the Electronic rods are also not recycled. Chemical waste like Aerosol cannot be recycled and should not be recycled. Bio medical waste like some needles should also not be recycled.

11. What do people think of them and the work they do?

Before the pandemic situation they were considered as non hygienic, dirty and felt disgusting. But this pandemic has made it the reverse. They are respected as of they are higher officers and in certain areas they are traditionally welcomed like offering garlands, etc.

12. Does their work pay them well?

No, they are not paid well to meet their basic needs. Their daily wages is about Rs.90 per day. How come this is sufficient to meet needs. It will be completely used in paying rent and a few needs. However they lead their families with this salary.

1.INTRODUCTION:

Pandemic as defined by the WHO – It is the world wide spread of a new disease in a particular time. This essay “Pandemic and Us" will let us know about the consequences of the spread of this novel corona virus in our country and worldwide too. This pandemic has reversed the whole world ultimately.

At the start of 2020, leaves were turned and we were armed with our new year’s resolutions. We never expected that by March we would be on lockdown by the government, only allowed to go out for essential items and to go to work if absolutely necessary. The origin of corona virus is said to be at a market in Wuhan, China. But the origin is not yet made clear by the scientist. Scientists are trying to unravel including the species which passed it to humans. It is said to be originated from bats. Though, the origination is not confirmed by the scientists, It has been killing almost the whole world (213 countries affected). This novel coronavirus has killed about 211000 people so far. People suffer from  breathlessness, brain damage etc due to its influence. Covid19 is not only a global pandemic but public health crisis too. It has also severely affected the global economy and financial markets. The major consequences are reduction in income, increase in unemployment, disruptions in transport, etc. Though it has affected the whole world, it had created a lot of benefits over the world such as decrease in air pollution, traffic,  etc. In this essay, we will be dealing with the positive and negative effects and devastative and productive effects of Covid19 on us.

2.CONSEQUENCES OF COVID19 ON HEALTH:

Since the beginning of this spread, health care providers are providing much support, solidarity towards the patients. They are even sacrificing their life for their country. Yet, many are being attacked by that tiny virus. The main organ affected by the virus is lungs. Other organs that can be affected include the heart and blood vessels, kidneys, gut, and brain. The virus enters the cells by binding to receptors angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 or ACE2 which are found on it. These receptors act as the site of entry and are found in the cells of multiple organs, making it easy for the virus to enter. It is said that the virus can make the blood cells to clump up and form clots(agglutination) which may lead to heart attacks and strokes. People with severe symptoms are treated in ICU(Intensive Care Unit) with ventilators to breathe. People affected by Covid19 also get affected by other attacks like physical assaults, psychological problems due to fear and humours, etc. Even though this virus leads to deaths, many people with high immunity power are surviving from this virus. In the recent research done in September,  India’s death toll is the third highest in the world after US and Brazil.

3. COVID19 AND THE GLOBAL ECONOMY:

                 A tiny virus has kneeled the world's leading economies. The infection has not only become a public health crisis, but has also affected the global economy. Reduced productivity, loss of lives, business closures,  trade disruptions and decimation of tourism industry are the major affects. It had caused a major decline in the economy globally. Comparing the Covid-19 death rate with the latest GDP data, we observe that countries that have managed to protect their population’s health in the pandemic have also protected their economy too. But only a few countries balance their economy and public health. Most of the countries had been adversely affected by the COVID-19 economically.  according to the IMF [ International Monetary Fund ], the global economy is expected to shrink by over 3% In 2020- the steepest slowdown since the Great Depression ,1929. In the US, Covid disruptions have left millions of people unemployed. In April , 20.5 million have been made unemployed. Globally, over 36 million are unemployed since March, which is almost a quarter of working age population. Furtherly, an analysis done by IMF has revealed that the manufacturing output in many countries have become void which reflects the great steep in the economy.

The IMF estimates that economies such as US, Japan, UK, Germany,  France,  Italy, and Spain are expected to contract this year by 5.9, 5.2 , 6.5, 7, 7.2, 9.1 and 8% respectively. Together they have expected to have a decline by 6% in 2020. The decline Is about 37.7% in the 1st quarter of the year.  In Asia, countries such as India, China, Indonesia, Japan, Singapore, S.Korea accounts for 13.5% in the first 4 months of 2020. In India, as per the data collected by the ministry of statistics and programme implementation,  the Indian economy contracted by 23.9% in April- June(the worst decline after 1996). India posted the steepest decline far worse than the US(9.1%) and Italy(17.7%) in the April- June quarter. Except for the Agriculture,  all the major sectors were badly hit. Economists suggest that India’s GDP would contract by 9-18% in this fiscal year. Even if the health crisis is managed by mid-2021, the economic recovery will be slow and uneven with adverse consequences on output, employment, and financial stability. 

4.IMPACT ON INDIAN AGRICULTURE:

The Agriculture of India has been adversely affected by COVID19 crisis and the resultant lockdown. The locust attack, amphan cyclone, earthquakes have added fuel to the fire. Agriculture, being the central pillar of the Indian economy, it serves the whole country, also exporting agricultural produce to the major parts of the world. Northern states of Punjab and Haryana have been adversely affected. When India’s nationwide lockdown was announced in March,  the harvest season of the rabi crops was upcoming which usually starts in mid- April.  It had been completely thrown-off balance, resulting in major liquidity issues. Labour scarcity had affected the supporting infrastructure around India’s agricultural sector. Storage units and milk processing plants are understaffed. Outside of these domestic woes, the Experts say that the range of export challenge unfolding. Lockdown have caused delays and backlogs in the supply chains. About half of million tonnes of Indian rice Is locked in supply chains. It should be noted that 22% of deaths have occurred in Bengal due to food supply disruptions. Farmers all across the country are wondering over their feet and of why God is treating them in such a way. This is not a happy time for the people who have invested in this sector and whose source of income highly depend upon Agriculture. No matter what happens, the poor sections of the society are always the hardest hit. 85% of the Indian farm household come under small and marginal farming activities. They are usually Landless and are supposed to pay higher rents even during the time of lockdown.

INDIAN GOVERNMENT'S FUND: 

The Indian government have quickly responded to the crisis and have announced $22 billion package which includes food and cash transfers. The relief package “Pradhan Mantri Garib Kalyan Yojana”( Prime Minister's plan for well-being of the poor).

It is aimed at the basic needs of the people who had been hit the hardest by COVID19 lockdown. But, Esther Duflo and Abhijit Banerjee, Nobel prize economists, say that the government should have been bolder with the package’s schemes. This is much low than the packages passed by US and other European and Asian countries. This package ($22 billion) is just 0.85% of India's GDP. It should at least spend 4-5% of GDP.

5.UNEMPLOYMENT CRISIS  IN INDIA AND ITS CONSEQUENCES:

India will take years to recover from unemployment crisis in India. Job losses and economic malaise in India show no sign of slowing as the impact of the corona virus continues to batter almost every sector. Experts and economists say that the recovery would take a long period and it is very difficult to take place. As many as 21 million people have lost their jobs till August (report by CMIE – Centre for monitoring Indian economy). Economists suggest that swift recovery looks unlikely. The virus's legacy will be with us “for decades", CMIE says. In India, the proportion of young workers affected by the crisis appears to be 40% of those 20-29 years(report by CMIE). It affects the next generation too. “If the youth were unemployed,  then the demographic dividend will become demographic demon” as said by the CEO of CMIE. India has not made any specific packages or measures for the unemployed people, when compared to US. Today, 28% of the youth in India belong to the age of 20-30 years, but half of them have less than secondary education or illiterate leaving only 13% as graduates. Most of the youth have less employable skills and they are not employed well (especially during this crisis). “This situation is really distressing because our demographic dividend, which was never quite properly exploited, is now getting into a different kind of logjam. This has long term implications” says the CEO of CMIE, Mahesh Vyas.

The currently unemployed IT staffs and other workers have got into Agriculture and allied activities, daily wages work, selling tender coconut and selling fruits and vegetables on roadside shops. This is due to the dried up job opportunities in the rest of the economy.  India’s current recession is expected to run through the 2nd half of the fiscal year.

"The pandemic had only made a sluggish economy and hastened the downslide”

6.ONLINE EDUCATION:

The shutdown has resulted over 1.2 billion children to be out of classroom. As a result, education had changed dramatically. Distinctive rise of e-learning have undertaken, where teaching occurs remotely and on digital platforms. Recent searches reveal that online learning make students to gain more information in a lesser time. There are currently more than 1.2 billion children in 186 countries affected by the school closures due to this pandemic. In response to significant demand, many online learning platforms are offering free access to their services, like BYJU's , Unacademy, etc. They have become most highly valued ed-tech company.

  Zoom app has become the biggest educating platform(especially in India). Eric Yuan, the founder and CEO of Zoom video communications, has made over $12 billion since March. He says that India is our largest market after USA. Google meet have also given much pecuniary value to the Google’s income.

There are, however challenges to overcome. Some students without reliable internet access and technology, struggle to learn digitally.

For instance, 95% of students in Switzerland, Norway, Austria, have a computer/ laptop which could be used for e-learning.  Whereas only 34% in Indonesia do and only 20% in India do(Research by OECD). For those who have access to better technology,  learning online is effective. But what about those who have little access to internet and technology? They are left behind. Some research shows that, on average, students retain 25-60% more material while learning online compared to only 8-10% in a classroom. E- learning requires 50-60% less time to learn than in a traditional classroom. On the other hand, students with low network access feel operosely. What has been made clear through this pandemic is the importance of disseminating of  knowledge across borders and all parts of the society.

7.ENVIRONMENTAL IMPACT :

Before the COVID19 issues, all over the world was suffering due to a higher level of urban air pollution especially in the form of SO2,CO2, NO2 , etc. The lockdown due to the Covid-19 issues became the opportunity for the earth to build a clear sky and clean air. This period is “the healing time of the nature”. The pandemic brought about a dramatic decline in the Industrial activities,  traffic, pollution. Restricted human involvement has become a blessing for the nature.  

Reports say that environmental conditions including air quality and water quality is improving and wildlife is blooming.

INDIGENOUS IMPACT :

India, has always been a place with immense population, heavy traffic and pollution causing industrial activities which lead to higher air quality index(AQI)values  in all metropolitans. But this lockdown has turned over everything.  The pollution level which we were not able to achieve during last decade, even after applying all sort of technological advancement,  have come into reality due to lockdown. The quality of air has started to improve and all other environmental parameters have improved.  Satellite images of Indian atmosphere also reflects the same trend of reduction in air pollution after Covid-19 outbreak. Major impact of lockdown can be observed on air quality, which is being experienced by everyone and recorded in various reports.  Smog has given way to blue skies in Delhi and other cities. To study these changes in atmosphere, Air Quality Index(AQI) and satellite images are being used. The lower the AQI value, the better is the air. Reports say that the index value has decreased by 33% in this lockdown. This lockdown has given the nature “a healing time” with reduced human interference in environment. Articles and reports in dailies reveal the improvement in quality of river too. The principal cause is the lack of industrial effluent entering the rivers due to lockdown. The other factors contributing to the improved quality of rivers are reduction of religious and cultural activities like Pooja, bathing, cremations on the river banks. The ozone depleting compounds which was not regulated 30 years, is being regulated during this lockdown. The ozone depleting compounds have also decreased by 35%. CAMS(Copernicus Atmospheric services) announced that the largest hole, ever seen in the ozone layer over the arctic, had closed in this lockdown due to the decrease in the pollution and depleting compounds. Scientists found the average surface ozone concentration increased by a factor of 1.5-2. The WMO states that due to the decrease in the economic activity, CO2 emission have  also decreased which also contributes to the closure of ozone hole.

“The undeniable benefit of this outbreak is that it showed us a better future that we all want to embrace. It gave us a preview of an environment that we all aspire to live in”

In the picture,  Rosanne Cash was not probably trying to pressure anyone into writing King Lear. Sir Isaac Newton discovered Laws of Gravity and invented calculus under quarantine.
